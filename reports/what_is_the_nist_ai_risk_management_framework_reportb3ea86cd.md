# What is the NIST AI Risk Management Framework?

**Research identifier:** report-b3ea86cd7e6d

## Findings

**Retrieved evidence (NIST AI RMF indexed sources):**

- The NIST AI Risk Management Framework (AI RMF) is a living document that is intended to be updated and improved over time based on technology evolution, global standards, and community feedback, with ongoing alignment to international standards and practices.
- The Framework is divided into two parts:
  - **Part 1** covers how organizations can frame AI-related risks and describes the intended audience, along with an analysis of AI risks and trustworthiness.
  - **Part 2** contains the Framework Core, which specifies four functions to help organizations address AI risks in practice.
- Trustworthy AI systems, per the Framework's trustworthiness analysis, should be:
  - Valid and reliable
  - Safe
  - Secure and resilient
  - Accountable and transparent
  - Explainable and interpretable
  - Privacy enhanced
  - Fair, with harmful biases managed
- The Framework Core describes four functions:
  - **GOVERN**
  - **MAP**
  - **MEASURE**
  - **MANAGE**
  These functions are further broken down into categories and subcategories.
- **Profiles** are used to tailor the Framework:
  - A **Current Profile** shows how AI is currently managed and its related risks in terms of current outcomes.
  - A **Target Profile** indicates the outcomes needed to achieve desired AI risk management goals.
  - Comparing the two reveals gaps that can be addressed through action plans, with prioritization driven by user needs and risk management processes.
  - Cross-sectoral profiles cover risks of models or applications used across use cases or sectors, as well as common governance, mapping, measuring, and managing of risks for activities such as large language models or cloud services.
  - The Framework does not prescribe profile templates, allowing flexibility in implementation.
- For security and privacy risk considerations within AI trustworthiness, organizations may leverage related NIST frameworks such as the Cybersecurity Framework, Privacy Framework, Risk Management Framework, and Secure Software Development Framework. These are outcome-based and structured around functions, categories, and subcategories similar to the AI RMF, and may inform security and privacy aspects of the MAP, MEASURE, and MANAGE functions, though they do not comprehensively address all AI system risks.
- Effectiveness evaluation of the AI RMF, including ways to measure improvements in AI trustworthiness, is described as a planned future NIST activity with the AI community. Organizations are encouraged to periodically assess whether the Framework has improved their AI risk management ability across policies, processes, and outcomes.

**Inference:**

- The NIST AI RMF is best understood as a voluntary, outcome-oriented risk management structure rather than a prescriptive compliance mandate. Its value lies in (a) framing AI risk and trustworthiness, (b) providing reusable functions for governing and managing AI risk, and (c) enabling organization-specific profiling to close risk-management gaps.

**Recommendation:**

- Organizations adopting the NIST AI RMF should begin by establishing a Current Profile, defining a Target Profile aligned to their AI use cases, and using the GOVERN–MAP–MEASURE–MANAGE functions to drive gap remediation. Related NIST frameworks should be consulted for security and privacy detail.

## Evidence Limitations

- The indexed evidence describes the AI RMF's structure, trustworthiness characteristics, Core functions, and profiles, but does **not** include detailed definitions of each category/subcategory under GOVERN, MAP, MEASURE, and MANAGE.
- The indexed evidence does **not** mention "tiers" as part of the NIST AI RMF functions, Core, or profiles. (Note: tiers are a feature of the NIST Cybersecurity Framework; their absence here may reflect indexing scope rather than the full published document.)
- The indexed evidence does not provide the full text of Part 1 audience analysis or the complete trustworthiness discussion beyond the listed characteristics.
- No internet or non-indexed documents were used; all claims are grounded only in the indexed knowledge base.

## Confidence

**Moderate-to-high** for the structural description of the NIST AI RMF (two parts, trustworthiness characteristics, four Core functions, and profiles). **Lower** for operational detail of individual subcategories and for any tier-based maturity model, because those elements were not present in the retrieved indexed evidence.
